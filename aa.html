When Your Immune System
Attacks Your Own Hair

Alopecia areata is a fascinating autoimmune disorder — your body's defense system, which is supposed to protect you, turns against your hair follicles. Understanding it means understanding some of the most important concepts in A&P: homeostasis, feedback loops, and what happens when they go wrong.

This module is entirely dedicated to the autoimmune type of hair loss — a fundamentally different mechanism from hormonal hair loss. No DHT, no androgen receptors. This is the immune system.

Key Facts About Alopecia Areata

Affects ~2% of the global population — no gender preference, can occur at any age.
Presents as patchy, well-defined round areas of sudden hair loss on the scalp or body.
Can progress to alopecia totalis (entire scalp) or alopecia universalis (entire body).
Unlike androgenetic alopecia, the follicle is not destroyed — it is forced into telogen. Regrowth is possible.
First FDA-approved systemic treatment (baricitinib) arrived only in June 2022.

Immune Privilege — The Set Point

Your Hair Has a Secret Forcefield

Imagine your hair follicles are like little castles. Normally they have a magic invisibility cloak that makes the immune system (your body's army) not notice them. The army walks right past and leaves the castles alone. That's the set point — cloaked and safe, hair growing normally.

The science: Hair follicles are immune-privileged sites — they actively suppress local immune surveillance. Mechanisms include: low MHC class I molecule expression on follicle cells (making them "invisible" to cytotoxic T cells), local secretion of immunosuppressive molecules (TGF-β, α-MSH, ACTH), and exclusion of activated immune cells from the follicle bulb. This privilege is necessary because rapidly dividing matrix cells expressing melanin antigens would otherwise be targeted by the immune system.

Stimulus — The Cloak Breaks

Something Breaks the Forcefield

One day — maybe because of a virus, a big stressful event, or sometimes for no clear reason at all — the invisibility cloak breaks. Suddenly the hair castle is visible. The army sees it. And the army thinks: "Wait — what IS that thing? It doesn't look like it belongs here. ATTACK!"

The science: The stimulus in alopecia areata is immune privilege collapse. The follicle begins upregulating NKG2D ligands (MICA, MICB, ULBP3) on its surface — stress molecules that signal "I'm damaged or foreign." MHC class I expression also increases, presenting follicle-specific antigens. Triggers include viral infections, physical stress, psychological stress, and genetic predisposition (HLA-DR alleles, ULBP3 gene variants) — but in many cases no clear trigger is identified.

Receptor — The Army's Eyes

Special Scouts Who Spot the Problem

Your immune army has special soldiers called CD8+ T cells. They carry tiny binoculars called NKG2D receptors. When the forcefield broke, the hair castle started glowing with special "LOOK AT ME" signals. The NKG2D binoculars see those signals and tell the soldier: "Found one. Move in."

The science: CD8+ cytotoxic T cells bearing NKG2D receptors are the primary effector cells in alopecia areata. NKG2D is an activating receptor that binds stress ligands (MICA, MICB, ULBP3) — molecules upregulated on follicle cells under stress. T cell receptors (TCR) also recognize follicle-specific antigens (particularly melanocyte antigens like Melan-A/MART-1) presented on MHC class I. IL-15 receptor on CD8+ T cells and NK cells is a second critical receptor that sustains their activation and cytotoxicity.

Control Center — The Signal Amplifier

The Radio Tower That Makes Everything Louder

When the soldier spots the castle, they send a signal called IFN-γ (a chemical message). That signal goes to a special radio tower inside the immune cells called the JAK-STAT system. The tower receives the signal and broadcasts it EVERYWHERE, making more soldiers wake up and join the attack. That's the control center — it takes the signal and turns up the volume.

The science: The JAK-STAT pathway is the intracellular control center. IFN-γ binds its receptor → activates JAK1 and JAK2 → phosphorylate STAT1 → STAT1 enters nucleus → transcribes pro-inflammatory genes (including MHC class I, NKG2D ligands, and more IFN-γ). IL-15 signals through JAK1/JAK3 → STAT5 → CD8+ T cell and NK cell survival and proliferation. This pathway is why JAK inhibitors are effective treatments — they silence the control center.

⚔️
Effector — The Soldiers Doing the Damage

The Army Actually Attacks

The effector is the one who actually does the job — the soldiers who storm the castle. In alopecia areata, that's the CD8+ T cells and NK cells. They march into the hair follicle and attack the cells at the bottom that make the hair grow. The hair factory goes into emergency shutdown — the hair falls out. But importantly, the castle walls aren't destroyed. Just the workers inside went home.

The science: CD8+ NKG2D+ cytotoxic T cells infiltrate the peribulbar region of the hair follicle (described as a "swarm of bees" pattern on pathology). They release perforin and granzyme B, inducing apoptosis in hair matrix keratinocytes. The follicle is forced into premature catagen/telogen. Crucially, the follicle's stem cells in the bulge region are spared — this is why regrowth is possible when the immune attack is suppressed. NK cells amplify the attack by producing additional IFN-γ.

↩️
Negative Feedback — When the Army Usually Backs Down

The General Who Says "Okay, Stand Down"

Normally, your immune system has a negative feedback system — a general who tells the soldiers "mission accomplished, go home." Special cells called regulatory T cells (Tregs) release calming signals that tell the army to stop fighting. In healthy people, this keeps the immune system from going too far. In alopecia areata, this braking system isn't working well enough in the scalp — so the attack keeps going.

The science: CD4+ CD25+ FOXP3+ regulatory T cells (Tregs) are the primary negative feedback in immune responses. They secrete IL-10 and TGF-β, which suppress effector T cell activity. In alopecia areata, Treg numbers and function in the perifollicular area are reduced. The follicle's own immunosuppressive molecules (TGF-β, α-MSH) are overwhelmed. Once immune privilege collapses, the local immunosuppressive environment cannot reassert itself without intervention — the brakes fail.

Positive Feedback — The Loop That Won't Stop

The Microphone in Front of the Speaker

Here's the really scary part. When the soldiers attack the hair castle and damage it, the damage makes the castle send out even more "LOOK AT ME" signals. That recruits even more soldiers. Who attack more. Which causes more damage. Which sends more signals. Which recruits even more soldiers... It just keeps getting louder and louder, like a microphone next to a speaker. This is why patches of alopecia areata can spread if untreated.

The science: The positive feedback loop: IFN-γ from T cells → JAK1/2 → STAT1 → upregulates MHC class I and NKG2D ligands on follicle cells → more T cell recognition → more IFN-γ. Damaged follicle cells release DAMPs (damage-associated molecular patterns) → activate innate immune cells → more IFN-γ and IL-15. IL-15 → JAK1/3 → STAT5 → CD8+ T cell and NK cell survival and proliferation → more IFN-γ. This self-amplifying cycle explains why early localized AA can spread to alopecia totalis or universalis.

🧸 The Whole Story — Plain English

Your hair follicles are invisible to your immune system — they wear a special invisibility cloak. That's the set point. Everything's fine, hair grows normally.

Then something — stress, a virus, bad luck — rips the cloak off. The follicle suddenly has big flashing signs on it saying "SOMETHING'S WRONG HERE." The immune system's special soldiers (CD8+ T cells) see those signs and move in.

Inside those soldiers there's a signal amplifier called JAK-STAT. It takes the "attack" signal and blasts it out to recruit more soldiers. Those soldiers release a chemical called IFN-γ, which makes the follicle flash even more warning signs, which recruits even more soldiers... a spiral that feeds itself.

The soldiers storm the hair factory and tell all the workers to go home. Hair falls out in patches. The factory building is still there — just empty. That's why the hair CAN come back if someone stops the attack in time.

And that's exactly what JAK inhibitors do — they jam the radio tower (JAK-STAT) so the amplified attack signal can't spread. The soldiers stand down. The factory reopens. Hair grows back.

Chapter 1

Immune Privilege: The Body's "No-Go Zone"

Before we can understand what goes wrong in alopecia areata, we need to understand something remarkable: hair follicles are normally hidden from the immune system. This isn't an accident — it's an active, carefully maintained biological state.

🛡️

What Is Immune Privilege?

Certain body sites — the eye, brain, testes, and hair follicles — actively suppress immune responses within their boundaries. This is called immune privilege. It's not that these sites have no immune activity; they actively produce molecules that discourage it.

❓

Why Do Hair Follicles Need This?

Hair matrix cells divide rapidly and express melanin-producing enzymes. Melanocyte antigens (like Melan-A/MART-1) can look "foreign" to the immune system — similar to how tumor antigens look. Without immune privilege, the immune system would attack every actively growing follicle.

Mechanism 1

Low MHC Class I Expression

MHC class I molecules present intracellular proteins to CD8+ T cells for inspection. Hair follicle bulb cells keep MHC class I expression very low — making themselves nearly invisible to cytotoxic T cells. No display = no target.

Mechanism 2

Local Immunosuppressive Signals

Follicle cells actively secrete: TGF-β (suppresses T cell activation), α-MSH (melanocyte-stimulating hormone — anti-inflammatory), and ACTH. These create a chemical "do not attack" zone around the bulb.

Mechanism 3

Physical Immune Cell Exclusion

The peribulbar region normally contains very few activated immune cells. A combination of basement membrane integrity and local chemokine suppression keeps effector T cells from accumulating near the follicle matrix.

The Key Takeaway

Immune privilege is not a passive state — it requires constant active maintenance. Think of it as a force field that needs power to stay on. When something disrupts the power supply (the stimulus), the force field drops instantly, and the immune system — which was always nearby — can suddenly see and attack the follicle.

Chapter 2

Set Point & Normal Range

In alopecia areata, the homeostatic variable being regulated isn't a simple number like blood glucose. It's a biological state: the immune privilege of the hair follicle.

🎯

Set Point

The set point is: follicle immune privilege maintained. In this state — low MHC class I, low NKG2D ligands, local TGF-β and α-MSH active — the hair cycle proceeds normally. Anagen runs for years, catagen and telogen follow in order. The immune system does not interfere.

📊

Normal Range

Small, transient fluctuations in immune activity near follicles are normal and handled by local regulatory mechanisms. The normal range is: occasional passing immune cell surveillance, quickly dampened by the follicle's immunosuppressive environment. No T cell accumulation, no sustained IFN-γ signal.

Why This Set Point Matters for Treatment

Because the set point is a state (immune privilege) rather than a number, returning to the set point means restoring the follicle's ability to hide from the immune system. JAK inhibitors do not restore immune privilege directly — they suppress the immune attack to give the follicle a chance to re-establish it. This is why AA often returns after stopping JAK inhibitors (~50% relapse within 6–8 months). The set point hasn't truly been reset; the attack has just been paused.

Chapter 3

The Stimulus

Something disrupts the follicle's immune privilege. This disruption is the stimulus that triggers the entire cascade. In alopecia areata, the stimulus is the follicle becoming visible — and vulnerable.

⚡

Stimulus (definition)

Any change detected by a receptor that initiates a feedback response. In AA, the stimulus is immune privilege collapse — the follicle begins expressing molecules that flag it as a target for immune attack.

Signal 1

NKG2D Ligand Upregulation

Under stress, follicle cells upregulate MICA, MICB, and ULBP3 on their surface. These are "I'm in distress" flags that activate NKG2D receptors on CD8+ T cells and NK cells — telling them to attack.

Signal 2

MHC Class I Upregulation

Follicle cells begin displaying their internal proteins on MHC class I molecules. This exposes follicle-specific antigens (especially melanocyte antigens like Melan-A) to CD8+ T cell receptors — creating direct targets for cytotoxic killing.

Known Triggers

What Breaks Immune Privilege?

Identified triggers include: viral infections (especially EBV, CMV), severe psychological stress, physical trauma, thyroid disease, and other autoimmune conditions. Genetic factors (HLA-DR alleles, ULBP3/MICA polymorphisms) increase susceptibility. In many cases, no trigger is ever identified.

The Stimulus Is a Loss of Identity

In most feedback loops, the stimulus is a change in a measurable quantity (too much glucose, too much heat). In AA, the stimulus is more subtle: the follicle stops suppressing its own visibility. It's less like a fire alarm going off and more like a camouflaged soldier accidentally removing their camouflage. The immune system was already nearby — now it can see the target.

Chapter 4

The Receptor

Multiple receptor systems detect the follicle's distress signals. In alopecia areata, the receptors are on immune cells — not on the follicle itself.

👂

Receptor (definition)

A structure that detects a specific stimulus and generates a signal. In AA, receptors are on the immune cells that will carry out the attack — they detect the follicle's distress molecules and generate activation signals.

Primary Receptor

NKG2D Receptor

Expressed on CD8+ cytotoxic T cells and NK cells. Detects MICA, MICB, and ULBP3 stress ligands displayed on follicle cells. NKG2D activation is a co-stimulatory signal that strongly amplifies T cell and NK cell cytotoxicity — like a second key in the ignition that makes the attack much more powerful.

Secondary Receptor

T Cell Receptor (TCR)

The TCR on CD8+ T cells recognizes specific peptide antigens presented on MHC class I. In AA, TCRs have been identified that recognize melanocyte antigens — Melan-A/MART-1 and TRP-1. These autoreactive T cell clones are the specific "soldiers" that home in on follicles.

Amplification Receptor

IL-15 Receptor (IL-15Rβγ)

IL-15 is secreted by perifollicular cells and keratinocytes. It binds the IL-15 receptor on CD8+ T cells and NK cells, driving their survival, proliferation, and cytotoxicity. This is the receptor that sustains the attack over time — even after the initial NKG2D signal fades.

Three Locks — Three Keys

The immune attack on the follicle requires multiple receptor-ligand pairs to fully activate: NKG2D + stress ligands (the "danger signal"), TCR + MHC class I peptide (the "this specific thing is a target" signal), and IL-15R + IL-15 (the "keep going" survival signal). This is why JAK inhibitors that block IL-15 signaling (like ritlecitinib, which targets JAK3 — the kinase downstream of IL-15R) are particularly effective in AA.

Chapter 5

The Control Center

Once the receptors detect the follicle's distress signals, intracellular machinery takes over. The JAK-STAT signaling pathway is the control center — it processes the receptor signal and decides what genes to turn on.

🧠

Control Center (definition)

The integrating center that receives receptor input, compares it to the set point, and directs the effector. In AA, the JAK-STAT pathway inside immune cells is the control center — it translates cytokine signals into gene expression changes that amplify the immune attack.

JAK1 / JAK2 pathway

IFN-γ → STAT1

IFN-γ binds its receptor → recruits JAK1 and JAK2 → they phosphorylate STAT1 → STAT1 dimerizes and enters the nucleus → activates interferon-stimulated genes: MHC class I (makes follicle more visible), NKG2D ligands (more "attack me" flags), and inflammatory chemokines (recruits more T cells).

JAK1 / JAK3 pathway

IL-15 → STAT5

IL-15 binds IL-15Rβγ → recruits JAK1 and JAK3 → phosphorylate STAT5 → STAT5 in nucleus → activates genes for CD8+ T cell and NK cell survival, proliferation, and cytotoxicity. This is the pathway that keeps the attack going long-term.

The Critical Point

Why JAK-STAT Is the Target

Both critical pathways (IFN-γ and IL-15) converge on JAK kinases. Block the JAKs → both STAT1 and STAT5 go unphosphorylated → the attack genes don't get turned on → the positive feedback loop breaks. This is the entire rationale for JAK inhibitor therapy in AA.

JAK-STAT: A 4-Step Signal

1. Cytokine (IFN-γ or IL-15) binds receptor on cell surface
2. JAK kinases (attached to the receptor) phosphorylate each other and STAT proteins
3. Phosphorylated STATs dimerize and move into the nucleus
4. STATs bind DNA and activate target genes → inflammatory response amplified

JAK inhibitors block step 2 — preventing all downstream steps from occurring.

Chapter 6

The Effector

The effector carries out the response. In alopecia areata, the effectors are the immune cells themselves — they physically attack the hair follicle and shut it down.

💪

Effector (definition)

The organ or tissue that executes the response commanded by the control center. In AA, the effectors are CD8+ cytotoxic T cells and NK cells — they carry out the physical attack on hair matrix cells.

Primary Effector

CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells

The main attackers. They infiltrate the peribulbar space (surrounding the hair bulb) in large numbers — pathologists describe this as a "swarm of bees" pattern around the follicle on biopsy. They kill matrix cells by releasing perforin (punches holes in cell membranes) and granzyme B (triggers apoptosis/cell death).

Secondary Effector

NK Cells (Natural Killer Cells)

NK cells amplify the attack by releasing large amounts of IFN-γ — feeding directly back into the JAK-STAT positive feedback loop. They also directly kill follicle cells via NKG2D-triggered cytotoxicity. NK cell activity is specifically targeted by ritlecitinib (JAK3/ITK inhibitor).

The Crucial Distinction

Follicle Is Forced Into Telogen — Not Destroyed

Unlike late-stage androgenetic alopecia (where follicles are replaced by fibrous tracts), in AA the follicle bulge stem cells are spared. The follicle goes into an extended kenogen (empty telogen) state. The factory is closed — but the building is intact. This is why regrowth is possible.

Why Does Hair Grow Back in Patches?

Not all follicles are attacked simultaneously — the immune response starts locally and spreads. Follicles at different stages of immune attack produce different sized hairs (or none). At the patch margin, you often see "exclamation mark hairs" — short hairs that are thinner at the base than the tip, indicating the attack started mid-cycle and the follicle began miniaturizing before shutting down completely. These are a diagnostic hallmark of AA.

Chapter 7

The Negative Feedback Loop

In a healthy immune response, negative feedback mechanisms limit the attack and return the system to homeostasis. In alopecia areata, these mechanisms exist — they just fail to dominate at the follicle level.

↩️

Negative Feedback (definition)

A response that opposes and reduces the original stimulus, returning the system toward the set point. In immune responses, negative feedback prevents the immune system from attacking indefinitely or targeting the wrong tissues.

The normal immune response has negative feedback built in — regulatory T cells, IL-10, and TGF-β all work to shut down immune activity after a threat is cleared. In alopecia areata, these brakes are overwhelmed by the positive feedback loop that amplifies the attack.

The Negative Feedback That Normally Works

Regulatory T cells (Tregs) — CD4+CD25+FOXP3+ cells that suppress effector T cell activity via IL-10 and TGF-β. Treg numbers are reduced in the perifollicular area in AA.

IL-10 — an anti-inflammatory cytokine that normally signals "stand down." Reduced in AA lesions.

The follicle's own TGF-β and α-MSH — once immune privilege is lost, these signals are overpowered by the IFN-γ amplification loop. The negative feedback exists but loses to the positive feedback in a 1v1.

Why Does AA Spread?

Activated autoreactive T cells don't stay put — they circulate through the lymphatic system and bloodstream. Once primed against follicle antigens, they can recognize and attack follicles elsewhere on the scalp and body. This explains the progression from a small patch → alopecia totalis (full scalp) → alopecia universalis (full body). The positive feedback generates a systemic pool of follicle-targeting T cells.
