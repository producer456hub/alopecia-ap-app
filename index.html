ELI5 Mode 🧸

Explain It Like I'm Five

Big science words can feel scary. Let's break everything down with simple stories and silly analogies. Tap any card to see the grown-up science version underneath.

🍪

Think of Your Body Like a Cookie Factory

Your body is really good at keeping things "just right." Not too much, not too little — just perfect. It does this all day, every day, automatically. Scientists call this homeostasis (say it: HO-mee-oh-STAY-sis). It just means "keeping things balanced."

Show science ↓
🎯
Set Point

The "Just Right" Number

Imagine your mom says you can only have 3 cookies before dinner. Three is the "just right" number — your set point. If you eat more, something happens to bring it back to 3. If you eat less, something nudges you back up to 3. Your body has a "just right" number for almost everything — temperature, blood sugar, heart rate... and even how many hairs grow on your head!

The science: The set point is the target value a homeostatic control system maintains. For hair, the set point is approximately 100,000 total scalp hairs, with ~85–90% of follicles in the active growth (anagen) phase at any given time. This ratio is genetically programmed and maintained by a balance of molecular signals in each follicle's dermal papilla.
Show science ↓
📏
Normal Range

Close Enough Is Good Enough

Your mom doesn't freak out if you ate 2 cookies or 4 cookies. That's still pretty close to 3, right? The "normal range" is like that — a little window of "close enough." Your body is okay as long as things stay inside that window. It only starts freaking out and making fixes when something goes way outside the window.

For hair: shedding 50–100 hairs a day is totally normal. Your body doesn't panic. But if you're losing 400 hairs a day — that's way outside the window, and something's wrong.

The science: The normal range for scalp hair density is 200–300 follicles/cm². Daily shedding of up to 150 hairs is within normal range. The anagen:telogen ratio of ~85–90%:10–15% is the normal range for follicle phase distribution. Outside these ranges, homeostatic correction begins.
Show science ↓
👋
Stimulus

Something Pokes the System

A stimulus is just something that pokes or changes the system. Like your little brother sneaking one of your cookies — that's a stimulus! Something changed from "just right," so now the system has to react.

In hair loss, the "poke" is a chemical called DHT. Think of DHT as a tiny troublemaker that sneaks into your hair's little factory and starts messing with the machines. In alopecia areata, the poke is your own immune system — your body's "army" accidentally starts attacking your hair!

The science: In androgenetic alopecia (AGA), the stimulus is dihydrotestosterone (DHT) — a potent androgen derived from testosterone by 5-alpha reductase type II — reaching genetically susceptible dermal papilla cells. In alopecia areata (AA), the stimulus is immune privilege collapse, exposing follicle antigens to circulating CD8+ NKG2D+ cytotoxic T cells.
Show science ↓
👂
Receptor

The Ears That Hear the Problem

A receptor is like the ears of your body's alarm system. It's the thing that notices when the stimulus happens. Like a smoke detector — it doesn't put out fires itself, it just notices smoke and starts beeping.

In hair loss, the "smoke detector" is a tiny protein called the Androgen Receptor (AR). It sits inside the hair follicle cells and listens for DHT. When DHT shows up, the AR catches it — kind of like a catcher's mitt — and that starts the whole chain of events that leads to hair loss.

The science: The androgen receptor (AR) is a nuclear receptor protein encoded on the X chromosome (Xq11-12). DHT diffuses into dermal papilla cells and binds the AR with 3–10× greater affinity than testosterone. The DHT-AR complex then undergoes conformational change, dissociates from heat-shock proteins, dimerizes, translocates to the nucleus, and binds androgen response elements (AREs) in DNA.
Show science ↓
🧠
Control Center

The Boss Who Decides What to Do

Once the smoke detector beeps, someone has to decide what to do about it. That's the control center — it's like the boss of the operation. It hears the alarm, thinks about it, and gives orders.

In hair loss, the boss is the DHT-Androgen Receptor team once it gets inside the cell's nucleus (the command room). Together they flip switches in the DNA — turning off the "grow hair" instructions and turning on the "stop growing" instructions. It's like a manager walking into a factory and telling all the workers to go home early.

The science: The DHT-AR complex acts as a transcription factor in the dermal papilla nucleus. It upregulates TGF-β1, TGF-β2, and DKK-1 (anti-anagen signals) while downregulating IGF-1, FGF-7, and VEGF (pro-anagen signals). At the systemic level, the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis is the control center maintaining androgen levels in the normal range via classic endocrine negative feedback.
Show science ↓
🏭
Effector

The Worker Who Actually Does the Job

The effector is the one who actually does what the boss said. When the boss says "close the factory early," the workers actually pull down the shutters and go home. The effector creates the real, physical change.

In your hair, the effector is a tiny cluster of special cells at the very bottom of each hair follicle called the Dermal Papilla. When DHT tells them to, these cells send a signal to your hair: "Stop growing! Go to sleep!" And the hair does. Each time this happens, the hair wakes up a little shorter and thinner than before.

The science: Dermal papilla (DP) cells are specialized mesenchymal cells (50–200 per follicle) that control hair matrix keratinocyte behavior. Under DHT-AR signaling, DP cells reduce IGF-1 secretion, increase TGF-β output, and upregulate DKK-1. DP cell volume is directly correlated with hair shaft diameter — a miniaturized DP produces a thinner, shorter hair in each successive anagen cycle.
Show science ↓
↩️
Negative Feedback Loop

When Your Body Says "Whoa, Too Much — Cut It Out!"

A negative feedback loop means the body's response fights back against the change. It's like a thermostat. Room gets too hot? Thermostat kicks on the AC to cool it down. Room gets too cold? It kicks on the heat. The reaction is always the opposite of the problem — that's what keeps everything balanced.

🌡️ Your body does this with hormones too! If you have too much testosterone in your blood, your brain says "Okay, okay — ease up!" and tells the body to make less. This normally works great. The problem with hair loss is that the hair follicle part of the loop goes haywire — even when your body is doing its job correctly at the big level.

The science: The HPG axis is the classic negative feedback system: rising testosterone → detected by hypothalamic receptors → reduced GnRH pulsing → reduced LH from pituitary → reduced testosterone from gonads → levels return to set point. In AGA, systemic androgen levels are typically normal — the pathology is follicle-level hypersensitivity to normal DHT concentrations, not a failure of the systemic negative feedback.
Show science ↓
📢
Positive Feedback Loop

When Things Spiral Out of Control

A positive feedback loop is the opposite — instead of fixing the problem, it makes it bigger and bigger. Imagine you're yelling into a microphone near a speaker. The speaker makes your voice louder, which goes back into the microphone, which makes it even louder, which goes back in... SCREEEECH! That horrible noise is positive feedback. Nothing is stopping it — it just keeps building.

This is exactly what happens with hair loss! DHT shrinks the hair follicle. But here's the cruel part — the smaller and weaker the follicle gets, the more it reacts to DHT. So DHT shrinks it more. Then it reacts even harder. Then DHT shrinks it even more. Round and round, getting worse every time.

The science: DHT-AR activation in DP cells upregulates TGF-β, which acts autocrinally to induce more TGF-β production in neighboring DP cells. DKK-1 suppresses Wnt/β-catenin signaling, weakening each successive anagen cycle. The miniaturized DP shows increased AR expression — amplifying its own DHT sensitivity. Perifollicular PGD2-driven inflammation and eventual TGF-β1-mediated fibrosis create a self-reinforcing cascade that progressively drives follicles toward permanent miniaturization.

🧸 The Whole Story — In Plain English

Okay, imagine every single hair on your head has its own tiny factory underground. That factory's job is to grow one hair. It works for a few years, takes a short nap, then wakes up and does it again. Your body has a "just right" rule: keep about 100,000 of those factories running at once.

Now, in some people — because of the genes they were born with — there's a troublemaker chemical called DHT. DHT sneaks into certain factories (the scalp ones) and whispers to the manager: "Hey, close up early today." So the factory closes early, and the hair that came out is a little shorter and thinner than before.

Next cycle? DHT does it again. "Close up even earlier." The hair is even shorter and thinner. And here's where it gets sneaky: the more the factory shrinks, the more easily DHT can mess with it. The smaller it gets, the more sensitive it becomes. It's a spiral that just keeps going.

The body's normal "balance police" — the HPG axis — keeps DHT levels in the blood totally normal. But it can't stop what's happening inside the individual factories. The big loop is working. The little loop is broken.

And that's alopecia. The body trying its best, but a positive feedback loop inside the hair follicle winning the battle — one hair cycle at a time. The good news? Scientists have figured out how to interrupt that loop with drugs — which you can try in the Drug Simulator! 💊

Three Types of Alopecia We'll Cover

Androgenetic Alopecia (AGA) — male/female pattern baldness, driven by DHT and androgen signaling. The most common type.

Alopecia Areata (AA) — autoimmune attack on hair follicles. Demonstrates a powerful pathological positive feedback loop.

Telogen Effluvium — stress-induced mass shedding. Shows how a stressor can shift the system's set point temporarily.

Chapter 1

The Hair Cycle

Hair doesn't grow continuously. Each follicle cycles through three phases — anagen, catagen, and telogen — repeating throughout your lifetime. Understanding this cycle is the foundation for understanding what goes wrong in alopecia.

Anagen

Active Growth

The dermal papilla signals matrix cells to divide. Hair grows ~1 cm/month. Melanocytes produce pigment. About 85–90% of your follicles are here at any given time.

Catagen

Programmed Regression

Signals like TGF-β trigger apoptosis (cell death) of matrix cells. The lower follicle shrinks upward. Only ~1% of follicles are here at once.

Telogen

Rest & Prepare

The follicle rests and prepares for the next anagen. BMP signals keep stem cells quiet. About 10–15% of follicles rest here. Normal daily shedding: 50–100 hairs.

Key Molecular Signals

Pro-Anagen (keep growing): IGF-1, FGF-7, Wnt/β-catenin, VEGF
Pro-Catagen (stop growing): TGF-β1, TGF-β2, FGF-5, BMP2/4, DHT (in susceptible follicles)

Chapter 2

Set Point & Normal Range

Before a system can "go wrong," you need to understand what "normal" looks like. In homeostasis, the set point is the target value the body aims for. The normal range is the acceptable window around that target.

🎯

Set Point

The ideal value a physiological variable is maintained at. Think of it like a thermostat setting. The body constantly reads the current value and adjusts to return to the set point. It is genetically programmed and can vary between individuals.

📊

Normal Range

A window of acceptable values around the set point. The body doesn't need to be at the exact set point every millisecond — small fluctuations within the normal range are fine. Outside this range, corrective action begins.

How the Set Point Is Maintained

The 85:15 anagen-to-telogen ratio is actively maintained by a balance of molecular signals. The "anagen clock" inside each dermal papilla cell progressively accumulates inhibitory signals over years until they override growth signals — triggering catagen. This is a built-in biological timer.

In alopecia, this timer is disrupted. In androgenetic alopecia, DHT shortens the clock — each cycle's anagen gets shorter. The ratio shifts. The set point drifts toward more telogen, less anagen. Hair density falls below the normal range.

The "Anagen Clock" Analogy

Imagine a sand timer in each follicle. Normally it runs for 2–7 years before flipping (triggering catagen). DHT puts a hole in the timer — it runs out faster each cycle. After many cycles, what was a 5-year timer becomes 6 months. The resulting hair is tiny (vellus), almost invisible. This is follicle miniaturization.

Chapter 3

The Stimulus

A stimulus is a change in the internal or external environment that is detected by the body and triggers a response. It's the event that "starts" a feedback loop cycle.

⚡

Stimulus (definition)

Any change that disturbs homeostasis or activates a receptor. A stimulus can be chemical, physical, or biological. It does not have to be harmful — even normal fluctuations in blood glucose act as stimuli that trigger corrective responses.

AGA Stimulus

DHT Reaching Androgen-Sensitive Follicles

Dihydrotestosterone (DHT) is produced when 5-alpha reductase converts testosterone in the dermal papilla cells. In people with genetic susceptibility, these follicles have higher levels of the 5αR enzyme and more sensitive androgen receptors — so even normal DHT levels cause an outsized response.

AA Stimulus

Collapse of Follicle Immune Privilege

Normally, hair follicles are "invisible" to the immune system (a state called immune privilege). A trigger — stress, infection, or unknown factors — causes the follicle to suddenly display self-antigens. This is the stimulus that begins the autoimmune attack.

TE Stimulus

Systemic Physiological Stress

In telogen effluvium, the stimulus is a body-wide stressor: childbirth, major surgery, high fever, severe weight loss, or thyroid dysfunction. Cortisol and substance P surge, simultaneously pushing many follicles into telogen at once.

Why DHT Doesn't Cause Baldness Everywhere

DHT is present in follicles all over your body — but it promotes growth in beard, chest, and body hair follicles while inhibiting growth in scalp follicles (in genetically susceptible individuals). The response to the same stimulus is location-specific, determined by each follicle's genetic programming. This is a key A&P concept: the same signal can have opposite effects in different receptor contexts.

Chapter 4

The Receptor

A receptor is a structure that detects a specific stimulus and generates a signal. It is the body's "sensor." In alopecia, the receptors are molecular — proteins on (or inside) cells that detect hormones or immune signals.

🔬

Receptor (definition)

A cell or specialized protein that monitors a specific variable and generates an input signal when a change is detected. In physiology, receptors can be cells (like baroreceptors in arteries) or molecular proteins (like hormone receptors on cell surfaces or inside nuclei).

AGA Receptor

Androgen Receptor (AR)

The AR is a protein inside dermal papilla cells. DHT diffuses through the cell membrane and binds to the AR. This binding causes a shape change, and the DHT-AR complex moves into the nucleus to act as a transcription factor — directly changing which genes are expressed.

Gene location: X chromosome (Xq11-12) — explains why AGA susceptibility is inherited maternally

AGA Receptor (upstream)

5-Alpha Reductase (5αR)

Technically the enzyme, not a receptor — but it "receives" testosterone and converts it to the active signal (DHT). Type II 5αR is the primary isoform in scalp follicles. Men born without this enzyme never develop male-pattern baldness — a key clinical clue that led to finasteride development.

AA Receptor

NKG2D Receptor on CD8+ T Cells

In alopecia areata, CD8+ cytotoxic T cells have NKG2D receptors that detect stress signals (NKG2D ligands: MICA, MICB, ULBP3) displayed on damaged follicle cells. When NKG2D binds its ligand, the T cell is activated to attack.

AR = "Key-in-Lock" — But the Key Changes

Testosterone fits the androgen receptor like a key in a lock. DHT is a "stronger key" — it binds 3–10× more tightly and stays 10× longer. In AGA, the lock itself (the AR) is also genetically altered to be easier to turn (shorter CAG repeats in the gene → more sensitive receptor). So the problem is both a stronger key AND a more sensitive lock.

Chapter 5

The Control Center

The control center receives input from the receptor, compares it to the set point, and determines the appropriate response. It "decides" what to do with the signal.

🧠

Control Center (definition)

The integrating center that processes information from the receptor and directs the effector. In the nervous system this is often the brain. In endocrine systems it can be a gland. In cellular systems it can be a transcription factor complex inside a cell's nucleus.

Systemic Level

The HPG Axis

Hypothalamus → Pituitary → Gonads. The hypothalamus detects androgen levels in the blood and modulates GnRH pulses. Rising testosterone feeds back to reduce GnRH and LH — classic negative feedback that keeps systemic androgen levels in the normal range.

Cellular Level

DHT-AR Complex in the Nucleus

Once DHT binds the AR, the complex enters the nucleus and binds androgen response elements (AREs) in DNA. This is the local "control center" — it reads the DHT signal and decides which genes to turn up or down in the dermal papilla cell.

AA Control Center

JAK-STAT Pathway in T Cells

In alopecia areata, when IFN-γ binds T cell receptors, JAK1 and JAK2 enzymes phosphorylate STAT proteins. STAT proteins then enter the nucleus and activate genes for T cell proliferation and more cytokine production. This pathway is the control center of the autoimmune loop.

What the DHT-AR Control Center Does

In a susceptible dermal papilla cell, the DHT-AR complex commands the cell to:
↑ Upregulate: TGF-β1, TGF-β2 (cause catagen), DKK-1 (block Wnt/stem cell activation), IGFBP-3 (mop up IGF-1)
↓ Downregulate: IGF-1 (pro-anagen survival), FGF-7 (stimulates matrix cells), VEGF (blood supply)

Chapter 6

The Effector

The effector is the organ or tissue that carries out the response directed by the control center. It produces the actual physical change that brings the variable back toward (or away from) the set point.

💪

Effector (definition)

The muscle, gland, or tissue that executes the corrective response. In the classic thermostat analogy: the heater and air conditioner are the effectors. They receive instructions from the control center and physically change the temperature.

Primary Effector

Dermal Papilla (DP) Cells

DP cells are the master regulators of hair follicle behavior. A cluster of just 50–200 specialized cells at the base of the follicle. They signal to the matrix keratinocytes above them to either divide (anagen) or undergo apoptosis (catagen). The DP is the effector — it executes the response to the DHT-AR signal.

Effector Response in AGA

Follicle Miniaturization

Under DHT influence, DP cells respond by: reducing their own size and number, secreting less IGF-1 and Wnt, secreting more TGF-β and DKK-1. This shortens each successive anagen cycle. Terminal hair (thick, pigmented) progressively becomes vellus hair (thin, colorless, barely visible).

AA Effector

Hair Follicle Bulb (Matrix Cells)

In alopecia areata, the target of T cell attack is the matrix cells in the hair bulb. Unlike AGA (where follicles are miniaturized), in AA the follicle structure is largely preserved — the matrix cells are simply forced into telogen. This is why regrowth is possible when the immune attack stops.

DP Cell Volume = Hair Diameter

The size of the dermal papilla directly determines the diameter of the hair shaft it produces. A large, healthy DP → thick terminal hair. A shrunken, miniaturized DP → thin vellus hair. Critically, the miniaturized DP also becomes more sensitive to DHT — setting up the positive feedback loop you'll see in the next chapters.

Chapter 7

The Negative Feedback Loop

A negative feedback loop opposes the original stimulus — it works to return a variable to its set point. Most homeostatic mechanisms are negative feedback. The body's control of androgen levels is a classic example.

↩️

Negative Feedback (definition)

When the response to a stimulus reduces or eliminates the stimulus. The response "feeds back" in the opposite direction. This is stabilizing — it keeps variables within the normal range. Blood pressure regulation, blood glucose regulation, and body temperature regulation are all negative feedback systems.

The HPG axis is a classic endocrine negative feedback system. At the body level, rising testosterone tells the hypothalamus to reduce GnRH, keeping androgens in their normal range. At the follicle level, DHT signals through the androgen receptor to shorten the anagen phase.

The Negative Feedback Loop at the Body Level (HPG Axis)

Rising testosterone in the bloodstream is detected by receptors in the hypothalamus → GnRH pulses decrease → pituitary releases less LH → testes produce less testosterone → testosterone levels fall back toward the set point. This keeps systemic androgen levels in their normal range. The problem in AGA is not that systemic androgens are too high — it's that the scalp follicles are genetically hypersensitive to normal androgen levels.

Chapter 8

The Positive Feedback Loop

A positive feedback loop amplifies the original stimulus — it pushes the variable further away from the set point. These are rare in normal physiology (childbirth contractions, blood clotting) but are a hallmark of many diseases.

📈

Positive Feedback (definition)

When the response to a stimulus amplifies or increases that stimulus further. This is destabilizing — it drives a variable progressively further from its set point. In disease, positive feedback loops create "runaway" cascades.
